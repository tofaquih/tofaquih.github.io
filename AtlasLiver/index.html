Atlas of metabolites and pathways associated with hepatic triglyceride content in middle aged men and women


Abstract

Background Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is characterized by the pathological accumulation of triglycerides in hepatocytes and is associated with insulin resistance and atherogenic dyslipidemia. Thus far, the extent of metabolic dysregulation associated with hepatic triglyceride accumulation has not been fully addressed.
Methods To gain insight in the spectrum of metabolites associated with hepatic triglyceride accumulation, we performed a comprehensive plasma metabolomics screening (1363 metabolites) in middle aged men and women (N=496) in whom hepatic triglyceride content (HTGC) was measured by 1H magnetic resonance spectroscopy (1H-MRS). Metabolite-HTGC associations were performed corrected for sex and stratified for sex. To gain insight in the metabolic clusters and pathways associated with HTGC, both correlation-based networks as well as graphical metabolic pathway-based networks were identified.
Results Our analyses revealed that, corrected for sex, 118 metabolites were associated with HTGC. The number of associations was much higher in women (83 in women versus 35 in men) and further exacerbated by menopause. However, the direction of the effects was fully overlapping between men and women. Analyses of metabolite correlation networks using a Gaussian graphical model (GGM) revealed a cluster of interconnecting metabolites encompassing branched chain amino acids (BCAA) and xenobiotic metabolites, and a second larger complex cluster encompassing lipid metabolites. Pathway analyses based on a genome scale metabolic model (GSMM) untangled the metabolite connections seen in GGM using graphical metabolic pathway mapping and metabolite flux alterations associated with HTGC.
Conclusion In conclusion, the combined network and pathway analyses showed that: In men and women, the direction of the association of the metabolites with HTGC is identical, but for many of these metabolites the effect sizes are larger in women, resulting in a larger number of associated metabolites in women. HTGC is associated with extensive dysregulation of BCAA metabolism and dyslipidemia characterized by increased metabolite concentrations, particularly diacylglycerols. In addition, we identified associations with glutamate and metabolonic lactone sulfate. Our data and online resources provide an atlas of metabolomic associations and pathways affected by HTGC.

Methods

Gaussian Graphical Model (GGM)

Significant metabolites from the multivariable analysis and the sex stratified analysis were used to develop the GGM networks. All GGM models were created using sparse gaussian graphical model with graphical lasso using the glasso function in R (qgraph R package version 1.6.9). In addition, the Extended Bayesian Information Criterium (EBIC) tuning parameter was used and set to 0.5. Visualization and exploration of the models were created in Gephi and exported as an interactive HTML5 using the sigmaExporter plugin.
